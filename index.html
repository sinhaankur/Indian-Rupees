The US has been printing dollars for decades.
So why is the rupee falling, not the dollar?

THE INTUITION FAILS

More dollars in the world should mean a cheaper dollar.

For any normal currency, that's exactly what happens. But the dollar isn't normal. It's the world's plumbing — and when the world gets anxious, every importing country needs more dollars, faster than the Fed can print them.

NORMAL CURRENCY
Print → Weaken

Supply rises, value falls. Simple supply and demand.

THE DOLLAR
Print → Absorbed

Global demand soaks up the extra supply. Strength persists.

BASELINE

Fed balance sheet small. Oil at $30 per barrel. India just beginning to rise.

HOW USD PRINTING WORKS

When the Fed "prints," it doesn't run physical presses.

The dollar supply expands through the banking system — and the way it expands determines whether the rupee gets a boost or a beating.

STEP 1

The Fed creates reserves

The Federal Reserve buys US Treasuries or mortgage-backed securities from banks. To pay for them, it credits those banks' reserve accounts. New dollars exist that didn't before — this is "quantitative easing" or QE.

2008–2014: ~$3.6 trillion added via QE1, QE2, QE3
2020–2022: ~$4.8 trillion added in COVID response
STEP 2

Banks lend it out

With more reserves, banks can issue more loans. Each loan creates a new deposit — which is broad money (M2). One dollar of central bank money supports many dollars of credit money. The supply multiplies.

M2 in 2000: $4.9 trillion
M2 in 2026: $22.7 trillion (+360%)
STEP 3

Dollars search for yield

Cheap dollars flow toward higher returns — Indian equities, Indian bonds, emerging market debt. This is the "carry trade." Rupee strengthens temporarily as foreign capital floods in.

2003–2008: Sensex up 6×, rupee strengthens to ₹39
2010–2013: EM bond inflows hit record highs
STEP 4

Then the music stops

When the Fed signals tightening — or when global risk rises — that same capital reverses. Investors sell rupee assets, buy dollars, wire money home. The rupee collapses harder than it had risen.

2013 taper tantrum: Rupee crashed from ₹54 to ₹68 in months
2022 hiking cycle: Fed +525bps, rupee broke ₹80 first time
FACTORS AT PLAY

Six variables that decide whether the rupee bends or breaks.

1

US monetary policy

QE → rupee strengthens · QT → rupee weakens

The Fed's balance sheet is the single biggest driver. When it expands, dollars flood emerging markets. When it shrinks, dollars retreat to safety. Indian rates can't compete with US rates on a risk-adjusted basis.

2

Crude oil prices

+$10/barrel → ~₹0.50 rupee weakness

India imports 85% of its crude. Every barrel must be paid in dollars. A $10 rise in Brent adds roughly $15 billion to the annual import bill — all sourced from the rupee market.

3

FII / FPI flows

$1B outflow → ~₹0.10 weakness

Foreign portfolio investors hold $700B+ in Indian equities. Their buying-selling decisions move the rupee in real time. In 2026, $21B has already flown out.

4

Dollar Index (DXY)

DXY up → rupee down

The DXY measures the dollar against six major currencies. When the dollar is strong globally, the rupee weakens regardless of India's own fundamentals. Currently at ~98.3.

5

India's current account deficit

Wider deficit → more dollar demand

India imports more than it exports — the gap must be financed in dollars. A widening current account deficit means more structural dollar buying every month, regardless of news flow.

6

RBI intervention

Sells dollars → slows rupee fall

The Reserve Bank doesn't target a fixed rate but smooths volatility. It sells dollars from $728B in reserves. Effective short-term, but every dollar spent is a dollar not available for a future crisis.

THE SYSTEM

The puzzle resolves in three layers.

01

The dollar is the world's plumbing

Every cross-border transaction, every barrel of oil, every central bank reserve — the dollar is the default rail. Demand is structural, not optional.

  • ~90%of global FX trades involve the dollar
  • ~58%of central bank reserves held in dollars
  • Mostcross-border debt is dollar-denominated
02

The petrodollar engine

Oil is priced in dollars. Always. Everywhere. Every barrel forces importers to source dollars and exporters to recycle their surplus back into US Treasuries.

THE TRAP FOR IMPORTERS

India earns rupees. The world charges dollars for oil.

To pay that bill, India has exactly three ways to source dollars. Each one has a failure mode. When two or three fail together — like today — the rupee has nowhere to go but down.

STEP 2 · INDIA DOWNSTREAM

How the petrodollar loop reaches the rupee.

01
Households pay more

Fuel at the pump, gold, cooking oil, mobile phones — anything sourced from abroad immediately costs more rupees. Inflation rises even when domestic demand is weak.

02
Companies with dollar debt suffer

Indian firms that borrowed in dollars must repay more rupees per dollar. Interest costs rise mechanically. Refinancing becomes expensive even if rates haven't moved.

03
RBI burns the war chest

To slow the slide, the Reserve Bank sells dollars from its reserves. Effective in the short run, but every dollar spent is a dollar not available for a future shock.

04
The current account widens

Imports get costlier in rupee terms while exports don't immediately surge. The trade gap grows, which means even more dollar demand next month. The loop reinforces itself.

+
One offset: dollar earners gain

TCS, Infosys, Wipro, Sun Pharma — companies billing in dollars convert back to more rupees. Their earnings rise mechanically with rupee weakness. This is why the Nifty can hold up while households feel pain.

→
India's slow workaround

Rupee invoicing with Russia and the UAE, BRICS payment experiments, and direct-rupee trade arrangements all chip at the petrodollar's edges. None reverse the system. But over a decade, they accumulate into a real alternative.

How it works: Inside the dollar-centric system, India sits at the wrong end of every flow. Oil drains dollars out. The Iran conflict spikes that drain. Foreign investors withdraw at the same time, and a strong dollar globally amplifies each force. The rupee has no buffer that can absorb all four at once.
THE FOUR FORCES

Every dollar-demand stream is firing at once.

No single factor explains the slide. Four forces are stacking on top of each other in 2026 — and the rupee has nowhere to hide.

FORCE 1

Crude oil dependency

Every barrel must be paid for in dollars. India imports 85% of its crude. Bigger oil bill = bigger rupee selling pressure.

FIIs sell Indian assets, convert rupees back to dollars, wire money home. Each wave weakens the rupee — which scares the next wave out.

FORCE 4

Globally strong dollar

High US Treasury yields keep capital parked in dollar assets. The Fed staying tight means the dollar gets stronger against almost everything.

THE IMPACT CHAIN

How rupee weakness reaches your wallet.

A weaker rupee triggers three first-order effects — and each one cascades into something the household, the firm, or the central bank feels directly.

WEAKER RUPEE
₹95.96 / $1

Dollar debt burden

RBI burns reserves

State banks sell dollars to absorb excess demand.

Households pay more

Fuel, food, iPhones, foreign tuition.

Profits squeezed

Importers, airlines, oil marketers absorb cost spikes.

Forex cushion shrinks

$728B peak reserves, slowly eroding.

THE ONE UPSIDE

IT and pharma exporters quietly gain.

TCS, Infosys, Wipro, Sun Pharma earn most of their revenue in dollars. When they convert back to rupees, they get more rupees per dollar. Their reported earnings rise mechanically with rupee weakness — which is why the Nifty can hold up even when the currency slides. It doesn't help households. But it's why India tolerates gradual depreciation.

WHY IT'S MOVING THIS WAY

The euro is structurally weaker than the dollar in 2026 — ECB rates are below Fed rates, and the eurozone imports more energy than the US. But because the euro is itself a reserve currency, it doesn't collapse the way EM currencies do.

Versus the rupee story

Both currencies are weaker against the dollar this year — but the euro's −2.4% slide is gentler than the rupee's −6.0%, because Europe has the structural buffer of being a reserve issuer.

REAL-WORLD PROGRESS

What's already happening on the ground.

A

India–Russia rupee–ruble trade

Since 2022, India has been settling crude purchases from Russia partly in rupees and rubles. Volumes are small (~$5–8B annually) but the rail exists.

Bilateral · slow but real
B

Yuan oil deals (Saudi, Iran)

Saudi Aramco accepts yuan for select Chinese-bound shipments. Iran sells most of its crude in yuan and rupees. ~10% of global oil trade settles outside USD.

Material · expanding
C

Central banks bought gold

Net official-sector gold purchases hit record levels in 2022–2025. Russia, China, India, Turkey, and Poland all added meaningful tonnage. Gold is the silent reserve diversifier.

Steady · cumulative
D

BRICS Pay & CBDC bridges

Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa, plus new members are piloting a cross-border payment system. mBridge (BIS) connects CBDCs from China, HK, UAE, and Thailand.

Early · could compound
E

India's UPI goes cross-border

UPI is live with Singapore (PayNow), UAE, Bhutan, Nepal, Sri Lanka, France, Mauritius. A retail rupee rail outside the dollar system, growing transaction-by-transaction.

Retail · grassroots
F

The euro and yuan as side reserves

EUR is ~20% of central-bank reserves. CNY is rising slowly (~3%). Neither replaces the dollar alone — but combined with gold, they make 42% of reserves non-USD already.

Structural · permanent
